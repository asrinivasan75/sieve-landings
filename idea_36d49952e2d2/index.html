How it works

Three steps from lab notebooks to briefing package

The flow is designed around how CMC consultants already work — not a new process on top of theirs.

STEP 01

Ingest your raw source files

Upload ELN exports (Benchling, LabArchives), PDF batch records, Excel analytical data, and synthesis protocols. Files stay in your encrypted workspace; nothing is used to train models.

STEP 02

Auto-map to CMC pre-IND structure

The system maps extracted content to the FDA-expected pre-IND briefing structure — drug substance synthesis, formulation, analytical methods, proposed specs — flagging every missing section with a specific ask.

STEP 03

Consultant reviews, not rewrites

Your CMC consultant receives a first-draft narrative for each section with source citations. They spend their expertise on scientific judgment and FDA strategy — not reformatting batch records into prose.

Honest answers

Questions worth asking before you share your data

These come directly from the risks we've thought hardest about. We'd rather you read them than discover them later.

Honestly, that's a real risk — and we're not pretending it isn't. A large consultancy with 40 active engagements a year would have strong incentive to build this internally once they see it work. Our bet is that the FDA section-mapping templates, ELN integration work, and especially the feedback loops from real FDA pre-IND responses accumulate into a durable data asset that takes longer to replicate than it looks. But the truthful answer is: we need to get deep enough into active engagements — and accumulate enough actual FDA feedback — before that window closes. That's why we're moving to pilot immediately with teams we already know, rather than spending months on infrastructure.
This is the sharpest risk in the entire thesis, and we treat it as a first-order gate — not something to paper over with an NDA clause. What we're doing in pilots: (1) offering a customer-managed deployment option where data never leaves your infrastructure; (2) working through the CMC consultant as the trusted intermediary who already holds your data under their own NDA; (3) being explicit that your files are not used to train or improve any shared model. We won't know if data-sharing fear is a structural blocker until we ask the 12 teams we already know. If 3 of them decline even under those terms, we'll treat that as a kill signal, not a sales problem.
For a solo consultant with one pre-IND at a time, a 60% hour reduction looks like $30K of lost revenue — not margin expansion. The "more clients, same rate" logic only holds if they actually have more clients waiting. We've had this conversation directly with consultants in our network, and the split is real: some see it as a margin protector (they're capacity-constrained and turn down work), others see it as a threat to their model. Our honest answer is that the tool is more likely to be adopted first by consultancies running 5+ concurrent engagements, and by sponsors who are the economic buyer. We will not pretend this tension doesn't exist in the pricing motion section of our conversations.
