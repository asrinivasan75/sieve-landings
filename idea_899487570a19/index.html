Honest answers

Questions worth asking

The three concerns attorneys raise most — answered as directly as we can, including the parts we're still figuring out.

How is this different from CaseMark or WealthDocx, which also claim fast trust generation?

That's the right question to push on, and we won't pretend the claim "built with real paralegal judgment" is self-evidently true. What we can say specifically: the clause library was built by someone who spent years at a large T&E practice mapping client intake answers to Massachusetts, New Hampshire, and Connecticut-specific language — not adapting a generic legal document API.

The real test is whether a pilot attorney who has also evaluated CaseMark finds the output materially better on edge cases: co-trustee authority language, incapacity thresholds, real property funding clauses. We're asking exactly that question during our pilot. Until we have that comparison from a real attorney, the differentiation is our assertion — not proof. We'd rather say that clearly than oversell it.

What's my liability exposure if a draft from this tool contains an error?

Your liability posture is the same as when a paralegal drafts and you review. You are the attorney of record. You review the draft. You sign off on it. TrustDraft outputs a document for attorney review — it does not provide legal advice to the client, does not sign anything, and is not the attorney. This is the same model HotDocs and WealthDocx operate under.

That said: UPL risk is real, and we take it seriously. We've designed the output explicitly as an "attorney-review-ready draft" — not a finished client deliverable. Every document carries a header flagging attorney review flags. If a state bar ethics opinion emerges from our pilot attorneys that requires product changes, we will make those changes before expanding. This is one of our explicit kill criteria, not a footnote. We'd rather hear about a concern from one pilot attorney than from the ACTEC listserv.

Which states are supported at launch, and what if my practice is in California or Texas?

We're launching with a focused set of common law states — Massachusetts, Connecticut, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and New York — where the clause logic is well-mapped and the founder has direct drafting experience. If your practice is primarily in California, Texas, or Arizona, TrustDraft is not ready for you at launch. Community property states require materially different clause logic — separate property treatment, spousal consent provisions, community/separate property characterization — and we won't ship those until the clause library reflects actual community property drafting, not an adapted common law template.

We're naming this directly because a solo attorney in CA discovering the community property clauses are wrong after paying $40 is exactly the outcome we want to avoid. State coverage expansion is on the roadmap; we'll announce it when it's ready and tested.
