The flow

Three steps, no spreadsheets

Set your goal

Enter a specific target — lose 15 lbs, gain 10 lbs muscle — and a deadline. The app calculates your daily calorie and macro targets from that number, not from generic TDEE tables.

Snap any meal

Point your camera at a plate, bowl, or restaurant dish. The app identifies the food, estimates portion size, and logs calories + macros in under 30 seconds — no searching, no barcodes.

Get an adapting plan

Your weekly meal plan and 4-day workout split update each week based on what you actually logged, not what you planned to log. Miss two dinners? Next week's plan adjusts.

Honest answers

Things you should know before signing up

These questions come from the hardest criticism of the idea. We'd rather answer them now than after you've wasted a week.

That's the right question to ask, and we don't have a fully satisfying answer yet. The real competition is not "photo vs. manual entry" — it's this app's recognition accuracy versus Lose It!'s. If we're at the same accuracy, there's no meaningful wedge. Our hypothesis is that being built entirely around the photo flow (rather than having it bolted onto a database search app) lets us tune the model faster and design the correction UX better. But we'd be lying if we said that's proven. That's exactly what the beta is for.
Honestly: the field is currently at 60–75% accuracy for unstructured home-cooked meals, and we think you need ~85%+ before it feels trustworthy enough to stop second-guessing every log. We don't know yet whether we can clear that bar at launch — it may be a 12-month R&D problem, not a day-1 feature. If beta accuracy stays below 80%, we will say so publicly and hold the app back rather than ship something that erodes trust. The kill criteria in our plan are explicit on this point.
We can't promise you won't. The research is clear: ~90% of people quit calorie trackers within 2–4 weeks, and most of those apps — including ones with photo logging — don't solve that. Faster logging removes one friction point, but it doesn't fix the motivational and behavioral reasons people stop. The adapting meal + workout plan is our attempt at a second retention mechanism: if the plan changes week-to-week based on what you actually did, there's a reason to come back that isn't just accountability. Whether that's enough is something we have to test — our beta keeps anyone who can't log on 5+ consecutive days in week one off the "success" list. We're not claiming we've solved this; we're testing whether we can.
